# Some Creamer

Working notes and source material for understanding the Creamer transform and testing possible applications in water-wave theory.

## Scope

This repository is organized around three goals:

1. Understand what the Creamer transform is and why it is useful.
2. Extract the main kinematic and statistical consequences of the transform.
3. Explore possible applications, especially:
   - kinematics of short waves on long waves
   - time derivatives and surface velocities
   - whether high-frequency or high-wavenumber tails should be interpreted as bound-wave structure

## What We Have Established So Far

- The Creamer transform is best understood as a near-identity canonical transformation on Zakharov variables.
- Its main purpose is to remove the cubic Hamiltonian term `H_3` when three-wave resonances are absent.
- In the transformed variables, the retained dynamics is linear, while leading non-resonant nonlinear structure is pushed into the variable transformation itself.
- In one dimension, the Lie-transform form has a clear kinematic interpretation in terms of horizontal Lagrangian displacement.
- It performs especially well for short-wave/long-wave interaction, capturing packet shift, straining, and amplitude modulation as a geometric effect.
- The Taylor notes emphasize a stronger interpretation: high-wavenumber tails and cusp-like geometry may be dominated by bound-wave structure generated by the nonlinear mapping, rather than by freely propagating short waves.

## Immediate Research Questions

- How should the Creamer transform be written in the most useful form for calculation: Hamiltonian, Lie-transform, or 1-D kinematic form?
- How should one compute `\partial_t \eta` and related velocities once the physical surface is reconstructed from transformed variables?
- Which parts of observed high-frequency or high-wavenumber tails are genuinely free, and which are bound?
- Can the transform be used to build improved initial conditions for numerical wave simulations with reduced error-wave content?

## Suggested Workflow

1. Read the deep-water 1989 paper first.
2. Extract the 1-D formulas that connect the transformed variables to the physical surface.
3. Separate clearly:
   - geometry reconstruction
   - linear time evolution in transformed variables
   - recovery of time derivatives and velocities in physical variables
4. Then study the 1994 finite-depth extension.
5. Use the Taylor notes as an interpretation layer for tails, cusps, and bound-wave content.
