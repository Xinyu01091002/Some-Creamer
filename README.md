# Some Creamer

Working notes and source material for understanding the Creamer transform and testing possible applications in water-wave theory.

## Scope

This repository is organized around three goals:

1. Understand what the Creamer transform is and why it is useful.
2. Extract the main kinematic and statistical consequences of the transform.
3. Explore possible applications, especially:
   - kinematics of short waves on long waves
   - time derivatives and surface velocities
   - whether high-frequency or high-wavenumber tails should be interpreted as bound-wave structure

## What We Have Established So Far

- The Creamer transform is best understood as a near-identity canonical transformation on Zakharov variables.
- Its main purpose is to remove the cubic Hamiltonian term `H_3` when three-wave resonances are absent.
- In the transformed variables, the retained dynamics is linear, while leading non-resonant nonlinear structure is pushed into the variable transformation itself.
- In one dimension, the Lie-transform form has a clear kinematic interpretation in terms of horizontal Lagrangian displacement.
- It performs especially well for short-wave/long-wave interaction, capturing packet shift, straining, and amplitude modulation as a geometric effect.
- The Taylor notes emphasize a stronger interpretation: high-wavenumber tails and cusp-like geometry may be dominated by bound-wave structure generated by the nonlinear mapping, rather than by freely propagating short waves.
- A useful physical picture in 1-D is that the transform acts like a nonlinear horizontal remapping of surface points: a near-linear surface in parameter space becomes a nonlinear Eulerian surface after point redistribution.
- In that picture, the additional harmonics are interpreted primarily as bound structure, because they are generated during reconstruction of the physical surface rather than propagated as independent free modes.
- This helps separate two kinds of nonlinearity:
  - dynamical nonlinearity in the Hamiltonian
  - geometric nonlinearity in the map back to the physical surface
- Eliminating `H_3` does not remove second-order bound harmonics from the physical surface; it moves their origin from the evolution equations into the nonlinear coordinate map.
- The deep-water case appears special because the canonical transform admits a simple geometric interpretation in terms of Hilbert-transform-related horizontal displacement.
- The finite-depth case appears less special: the transform still exists, but the clean deep-water geometric picture does not survive in equally simple form.

## Immediate Research Questions

- How should the Creamer transform be written in the most useful form for calculation: Hamiltonian, Lie-transform, or 1-D kinematic form?
- How should one compute `\partial_t \eta` and related velocities once the physical surface is reconstructed from transformed variables?
- Which parts of observed high-frequency or high-wavenumber tails are genuinely free, and which are bound?
- Can the transform be used to build improved initial conditions for numerical wave simulations with reduced error-wave content?
- For intermediate depth, for example `kd > 0.5`, is removal of `H_3` still enough for a practically "best" mapping, or must one also absorb higher-order non-resonant Hamiltonian structure?
- Is the right finite-depth target still a canonical normal-form transform, or a more directly optimized physical-surface reconstruction map?
- Why does the deep-water Lie transform admit a simple remapping interpretation while the 1994 finite-depth transform does not?
- Can one define a depth-dependent "best mapping" by accuracy of crest-trough shape, short-on-long modulation, statistical simplicity, or numerical usefulness?

## Suggested Workflow

1. Read the deep-water 1989 paper first.
2. Extract the 1-D formulas that connect the transformed variables to the physical surface.
3. Prototype key algebra in [Mathematica](/c:/Research/Some%20Creamer/Mathematica):
   - series expansions
   - operator identities
   - reconstruction formulas
   - finite-depth asymptotics
4. Separate clearly:
   - geometry reconstruction
   - linear time evolution in transformed variables
   - recovery of time derivatives and velocities in physical variables
5. Then study the 1994 finite-depth extension.
6. Use the Taylor notes as an interpretation layer for tails, cusps, and bound-wave content.
7. Treat finite depth as a new regime rather than a small edit of the deep-water picture:
   - test where the geometric remapping picture still works
   - test where higher-order Hamiltonian terms become essential
   - compare "best canonical transform" against "best reconstruction map"

## Symbolic Workflow

- `wolframscript` is available locally, so symbolic derivations can be scripted and checked from the terminal.
- Mathematica is expected to be useful for:
  - perturbation series
  - Fourier and operator algebra
  - consistency checks for candidate mappings
  - finite-depth asymptotics
- It may be less effective as a full automatic Lie-transform engine, so the preferred strategy is to break derivations into smaller symbolic subproblems and keep the theory logic explicit in notes.
